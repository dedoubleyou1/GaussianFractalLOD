PriorSplat: Recursive Gaussian Priors for Object Reconstruction with Built-in Level of Detail

David Wallin
arXiv Preprint, 2026
Paper (coming soon) Code arXiv (coming soon)

Abstract

A 3D Gaussian can be viewed as a soft, distributional claim about a part of space: an assertion that some amount of occlusion and color exists, approximately in some area, distributed approximately like the Gaussian itself. We show that this perspective yields a principled prior for finer-grained Gaussians within the same area.

Building on this insight, we introduce PriorSplat, an object reconstruction method for bounded objects with known alpha masks. PriorSplat computes the coarsest prior — a single Gaussian summarizing the entire object — in closed form from silhouette statistics, then recursively refines it through gradient-driven subdivision. Each child Gaussian is parameterized as a delta from its parent, yielding a self-similar hierarchy with built-in level-of-detail, structural compression, and principled initialization at every scale.

The Core Insight

A 3D Gaussian is not merely a rendering primitive. It is also a statistical approximation of the appearance and occlusion contributed by an area of space. Viewed this way, a Gaussian is making a soft claim: there is approximately this much occlusion, of approximately this color, distributed approximately like this, in approximately this area. The softness is essential — a Gaussian has no hard boundary, and never claims one.

Once we adopt this view, a natural question follows: what relationship should hold between a coarse approximation of an area and a finer one of the same area? Our answer is that the coarse Gaussian acts as a prior for the finer set. It tells the finer level approximately where occlusion should sit, approximately how it should be distributed, and approximately what color to expect. The finer level does not start from scratch — it begins consistent with the coarse approximation and refines from there.

What the Prior Gives You

This single observation drives the entire system. Because every Gaussian is already a prior over its area, we get four benefits for free:

  • Principled initialization at every level. The coarsest prior — a single Gaussian summarizing the whole object — is computed in closed form from 2D silhouette moments, with no optimization and no local minima. Each subsequent level inherits its initialization from its parent through optimal subdivision formulas.
  • Structural compression. Child initializations are fully determined by parent parameters, so they never need to be stored. Only the deltas — the corrections to the prior — are saved.
  • Built-in level of detail. The hierarchy can be rendered at any depth, providing a single model that supports a continuum of fidelity-versus-cost trade-offs.
  • Adaptive allocation. Where the coarse prior is sufficient, no refinement is needed. Where it is insufficient — measured by training gradients — the model subdivides more aggressively.

This is in contrast to existing hierarchical Gaussian methods, which impose hierarchical structure on top of Gaussians as an engineering choice rather than deriving it from the statistical role Gaussians already play. Our hierarchy is not an external scaffold — it is a recursive refinement of a single, consistent statistical claim about the object.

Pipeline

PriorSplat trains in two phases:

Phase 1: Geometric root fitting. For each training view, we compute the 2D moments (centroid, covariance, total coverage) of the alpha mask silhouette. These moments define a 2D Gaussian per view, which we back-project into 3D via least-squares ray intersection and Voronoi-weighted averaging across viewpoints. The result is a single 3D Gaussian that summarizes the entire object — no iterative optimization required. A brief second sub-phase then fits spherical harmonics for view-dependent color on the frozen geometry.

Phase 2: Recursive refinement. Starting from the root, we train each level of the hierarchy at a resolution appropriate to its detail (rN = min(32 · √2N, rmax)). After each level, we inspect accumulated training gradients as a measure of where the current prior is insufficient, and split Gaussians in those regions — into two, four, or eight children depending on the gradient magnitude. Children are parameterized as learned deltas on top of initial parameters derived from the parent through optimal subdivision formulas, so they begin consistent with the parent's distributional claim and refine from there.
